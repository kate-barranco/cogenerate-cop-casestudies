The framework

The 3 Ps of cogeneration

CoGenerate's 3 Ps framework identifies three conditions that must be in place for intergenerational collaboration to take root and last. They aren't a checklist — they're mutually reinforcing. Each one strengthens the others.

P1Proximity
Who is already in your community?
Youngers and olders are often hidden in plain sight because society is age-segregated. Starting with people already proximate to your work creates the strongest foundation. More frequent, longer-term contact consistently leads to better outcomes.
P2Purpose
Why will each generation show up?
This is a human-centered design question. Youngers and olders may arrive with different motivations — and that's okay. What matters is understanding each generation's "why," naming where they overlap, and being honest about where they differ.
P3Partnership
How will roles and power be shared?
This is the most complex P — it involves power sharing, problem solving, and navigating conflict. Strong partnerships include intentional structure, preparation for both generations, and space for youngers to mentor olders, not just the reverse.
Why these three are mutually reinforcing: Proximity without purpose is just co-presence. Purpose without partnership collapses into adults doing the doing. And partnership without proximity lacks the time and trust needed to actually work. Use all three together — each one strengthens the others as your collaboration deepens.

Case studies

Organizations in action

Be Loud
New Orleans, LA · Youth radio & storytelling
Proximity

Started as a classroom microphone experiment, Be Loud now runs a youth radio program where young people interview community elders and elected leaders — with every conversation designed to flow both ways.

Challenge

How do you create genuine intergenerational dialogue — not just youth "being interviewed" or elders "delivering wisdom" — in a city where age-segregated spaces are the norm and connection rarely happens by accident?

Do's & don'ts
✓

Practice connection before expecting it. Use structured warm-ups — like skill-share speed rounds where everyone teaches and learns — before the "real" conversation starts. Don't just put generations in a room and hope for the best.

✓

Make adults prepare questions, not just answers. Require adults to arrive with questions for young people too. This shifts the dynamic, creates genuine dialogue, and gives youth a felt sense of being heard.

✕

Don't wait for perfect structure before starting. Organic, even messy, intergenerational moments build real relationships. Get people in the room first — then find the structure that formalizes what's already working.

Citizen University
National (locally anchored) · Civic power & leadership
Purpose

Citizen University runs national cohorts of adult civic catalysts and youth collaboratory students — and is now piloting a local model where youth and adult mentors collaborate on community power projects together, in place.

Challenge

Young people in national cohorts often return home energized but isolated — lacking the local relationships to sustain momentum. How do you connect purpose-driven youth with adults who share their vision, right where they live?

Do's & don'ts
✓

Look for youngers and olders hidden in plain sight. Age-segregated systems obscure intergenerational relationships that already exist in your community. Starting proximate creates a stronger foundation than recruiting from scratch.

✓

Use storytelling as the entry point. Before co-creating change, invite both generations to share what they care about and why. When adults are supported to listen without fixing, and youth feel genuinely heard, the collaboration that follows is more grounded.

✕

Don't assume shared presence means shared purpose. Adults and youth may show up with very different motivations. Naming those motivations — and finding the genuine overlap — is the design work, not a prerequisite to it.

Kentucky Student Voice Team
Kentucky, USA · Education advocacy
Partnership

A 100+ member youth-led organization that spun off from a 35-year-old adult-run nonprofit to co-create more just and democratic schools — with adults playing targeted, intentional supporting roles.

Challenge

How do you build a genuinely youth-led organization without losing the institutional knowledge, legal scaffolding, and relational continuity that adults provide — especially as youth leadership turns over year after year?

Do's & don'ts
✓

Name the invisible labor. Adults often quietly hold systems together — finances, liability, institutional memory. Making these contributions visible prevents resentment and single points of failure.

✓

Design adult scaffolding intentionally, then pull it back. Starting with more support and reducing it over time helps more young people participate meaningfully. It's a strategy for equity, not a power grab.

✕

Don't create youth-only spaces without also investing in intergenerational ones. Youth-only space is healthy — but if adults are excluded from building relationships, trust and continuity break down when leadership turns over.
